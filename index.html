Profile

I am a scientist driven by the challenge of reconciling human development with planetary boundaries. With a unique foundation of dual doctorates—one in Quantum Information (ICFO, Spain) and another in Earth System Science (Max Planck Institute for Biogeochemistry, Germany)—I approach climate challenges with both rigorous mathematical modeling and a deep understanding of ecological limits.

Currently serving as an Assistant Professor at Krea University, my work bridges the gap between hard physical science and socio-economic realities. My research spans atmospheric thermodynamics, the impact of aerosols on the Indian Monsoon, and critiques of traditional, GDP-centric development metrics. Ultimately, I aim to make climate science a tool for public empowerment, equity, and scalable sustainability.

A scalability-centric perspective on global human development within environmental limits Sustainability: Science, Practice and Policy (2025)
The Gist: What does it mean to be a "developed" country if that lifestyle destroys the planet? This paper argues that true development must be scalable to the whole world without breaking ecological limits. We show why countries balancing good living standards with low ecological footprints (like Costa Rica) are better role models for the future than traditionally wealthy nations.

Interpreting climate performance indices: implications for equitable and effective policy Climate Policy (2025)
The Gist: Popular "green" rankings often unfairly penalize developing nations while rewarding wealthy countries that actually cause more per-capita emissions. We unpack why these metrics are mathematically and ethically skewed, and propose ways to measure climate action that are actually fair to the Global South.
Download PDF
Future intensification of Northern Hemisphere monsoons due to declining remote aerosol pollution Environmental Research Letters (2025)
The Gist: Cleaning up air pollution is good for human health, but it has a hidden global side effect. As North America and Europe reduce their "aerosol" pollution, the Northern Hemisphere warms faster. Our research shows this shifting heat imbalance is projected to significantly intensify India's monsoon rains in the future.
Download PDF
A post-AR6 update on observed and projected climate change in India PLOS Climate (2025)
The Gist: An updated "state of the climate" specifically for India. Following the latest UN IPCC reports, we synthesized the most current data to provide clear projections on how temperatures, rainfall, and extreme weather events will change across the Indian subcontinent in the coming decades.

Bridging climate science, policy, and communities: collaborative pathways for climate resilience Frontiers in Climate (2025)
The Gist: Top-down science isn't enough to protect vulnerable regions. This paper highlights how scientists, policymakers, and local communities in the Indo-Pacific must collaborate—rather than working in silos—to build practical, on-the-ground resilience against climate impacts.

Compound weather and climate extremes in the Asian region Frontiers in Climate (2025)
The Gist: Climate disasters rarely happen one at a time anymore. We explore the rising threat of "compound extremes"—like simultaneous heatwaves and droughts, or floods combined with cyclones—across Asia, and offer concrete policy recommendations to manage these cascading, multi-layered risks.

Advancing South Asian monsoon climate change projections: Challenges and opportunities J. Ind. Geophys. Union (2022)
The Gist: Why are climate models historically bad at predicting the South Asian monsoon? This paper breaks down the technical roadblocks—from modeling microscopic clouds to local land-use changes—and maps out how the scientific community can improve these crucial weather forecasts.

Constraining Global Changes in Temperature and Precipitation Geophysical Research Letters (2020)
The Gist: Using fundamental laws of physics, this paper offers a new, simplified mathematical way to calculate how much the Earth will warm and how much more it will rain, based directly on how much extra energy the Earth's surface is absorbing from the sun and greenhouse gases.
